Determine your tax obligations

On this page:

  • What is treaty-protected property

What is treaty-protected property

You may own property that is exempt from tax in Canada because of a tax treaty between Canada and your country of residence. We call this treaty-protected property.

Most tax treaties only allow Canada to tax income or gains from:

  • Canadian real and resource properties
  • shares of companies that get most of their value from these properties

Because of these treaties, Canada cannot tax other types of property that the Income Tax Act (the act) would normally tax.

As of January 1, 2009, your property is treaty-protected if the income or gain from selling it is fully exempt under Part I of the act.

Selling Canadian property as a non-resident

If you are a non-resident of Canada selling property, you may need to apply for a certificate of compliance from the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA).

We can refuse to issue your certificate if you do not meet your Underused Housing Tax (UHT) obligations. This refusal can delay or impact the legal sale of your property in Canada.

Follow these steps to make sure you meet your requirements.

  1. Find out if you are disposing of taxable Canadian property

    Check if the property you are selling is taxable Canadian property. This includes:

    • real estate located in Canada
    • life insurance policies in Canada
    • resource or timber property in Canada
    • certain shares, trusts, or partnership interests tied to Canadian assets
    • business-use property in Canada

  2. Check if your property is excluded

    You do not need to apply for a certificate of compliance if you are selling an excluded property. These properties include:

    • securities listed on a recognized exchange
    • units of mutual fund trusts
    • certain insurance and banking assets
    • treaty-exempt properties

    Treaty-protected and treaty-exempt property

    Tax treaties may prevent Canada from taxing the income or gains on certain types of property. If a tax treaty between Canada and your country of residence fully exempts your property from Canadian tax, it is considered a treaty-protected property.

    Your property is considered treaty-exempt if both of the following apply:

    • it is a treaty-protected property when you sell it
    • the buyer notifies us (if you and the buyer are related)

    We do not issue a certificate of compliance for these properties. Instead, the buyer must send us a notification.

  3. Understand your Underused Housing Tax (UHT) obligations

    You must meet your UHT requirements before we can issue a certificate of compliance.

    1. File a return if you are an affected owner, even if you qualify for an exemption
    2. Pay the tax if you do not qualify for an exemption

    You must file a separate return for each residential property you own in Canada.

  4. Apply for a certificate of compliance

    To apply for your certificate of compliance, you must:

    • complete and submit the correct application form
    • attach the necessary supporting documents
    • pay any tax you owe
